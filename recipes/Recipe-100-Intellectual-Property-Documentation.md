## The Problem

Intellectual property—patents, trademarks, copyrights, and trade secrets—represents significant value, but documentation is often scattered, incomplete, or nonexistent. Companies struggle to inventory what IP they have, track registration status, document trade secrets, or prepare for IP due diligence. Poor IP documentation creates risk in M&A, licensing negotiations, and enforcement actions.

**Pain Points:**
- IP assets scattered across files, databases, and team members with no central inventory
- Unclear what IP the company actually owns vs. what was created by contractors or early employees
- Trade secrets not formally identified or documented, leaving them vulnerable
- Patent and trademark deadlines missed due to poor tracking
- Unable to quickly produce IP documentation for transactions or partnerships
- Difficulty demonstrating IP value to investors or acquirers
- Gaps in IP assignment agreements discovered too late to remediate

---

## The Outcome

Comprehensive, organized intellectual property documentation including inventories, registration tracking, trade secret identification, and ownership records—enabling proper IP management, protection, and valuation.

**What You'll Have When Done:**
- Complete IP inventory across all categories (patents, trademarks, copyrights, trade secrets)
- Registration and maintenance tracking with upcoming deadline alerts
- Trade secret register with protection measures documented
- Ownership verification and gap analysis for assignments
- Open source compliance documentation
- IP portfolio summary for stakeholders or transactions

## Troubleshooting Common Issues

| Problem | Likely Cause | Solution |
|---------|--------------|----------|
| IP scattered across departments | No centralized tracking | Create central register; assign ownership to legal/IP function; mandate updates |
| Missing documentation | Agreements lost or never created | Systematic search; recreate where possible; disclose gaps in transactions; remediate going forward |
| Trade secrets not identified | No formal program | Work with business units; use identification criteria; document systematically |
| Can't prove IP ownership | Assignment chain gaps | Trace development history; obtain retroactive assignments; document gaps for disclosure |
| Overwhelming to document everything | Trying to be too comprehensive initially | Start with critical assets; build systematically; improve over time |
| Information quickly becomes outdated | No maintenance process | Assign ownership; schedule regular reviews; integrate into business processes |

## Tips from Experience

1. **Centralize everything obsessively.** Create one source of truth for all IP. Don't rely on scattered files or individual knowledge.

2. **Document creation, not just registration.** Capture when IP was created, by whom, and under what circumstances. This matters for ownership and valuation.

3. **Review and update regularly.** IP portfolios change. Schedule quarterly reviews to keep inventories current.

4. **Train your team.** Ensure employees understand IP obligations, what's confidential, and how to protect trade secrets.

5. **Integrate IP into processes.** Make IP review part of hiring, product development, employee offboarding, and contract review.

## Building Your Repeatable System

After using this recipe 2-3 times, establish your system:

1. **Maintain central IP database.** Keep one authoritative source updated continuously. Don't wait for transactions to organize.

2. **Create standard processes.** Standardize how new IP is captured, how assignments are obtained, how trade secrets are identified.

3. **Implement regular reviews.** Schedule quarterly IP inventory reviews. Conduct annual comprehensive audits.

4. **Automate where possible.** Use deadline tracking systems. Set up automated reminders for renewals and maintenance fees.
